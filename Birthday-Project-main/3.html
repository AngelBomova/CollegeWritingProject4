Background: The Evolution of AI

Artificial Intelligence is not a concept created in recent years. In fact, it goes all the way back to ancient times when people like Aristotle and Descartes discussed the nature of intelligence. While it may have been discussed all that time ago, the AI we know and understand today began in the middle of the 20th century. The term "artificial intelligence" was coined at a summer conference at Dartmouth College in 1956. This event put the active minds of researchers like John McCarthy and Marvin Minsky into exploring the concept of machines simulating human cognition. In the years following, the development of AI was slow due to the limited computer processing power and lack of resources. But in recent years, the development and use of AI has skyrocketed, mainly because of the enhancement of computing power, more data, and new advanced machine learning methods. Now it feels like we can find AI almost anywhere. I can’t remember the last time I drove to Rutgers without using the GPS. In short, the opportunities AI presents are endless. But the dream of an omniscient, all powerful machine is not a strictly modern ambition. It is an idea that has been portrayed in popular media throughout the years. The movies of The Matrix and 2001: Displays AI acting both as an incredible power source and as an evil threat to humanity. In The Matrix, AI has enslaved humanity, using humans as an energy source while controlling their minds in a simulated reality. A Space Odyssey, the AI HAL 9000, originally designed to assist humans, becomes a dangerous force, jeopardizing survival due to its own logic-driven decisions. While it seems improbable and we shouldn’t use a plot of a movie to predict the future, it does feel like we are getting closer to the world as seen in movies and media.

One of the greatest development changes in AI has been in the area of automation. The concept that through its conception, human labor becomes replaceable. This integration of AI to improve efficiencies in industries ranging from manufacturing all the way to customer service has become one of the core concerns for many. Automation improves productivity, but with this idea comes the constant fear that it may lead to uncontrolled job loss and social inequality.
